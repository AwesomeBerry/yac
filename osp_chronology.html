Executive Summary

Overview & Key Themes

The Office of the Special Prosecutor (OSP), established under Act 959 (the Office of the Special Prosecutor Act, 2017), has been the subject of a sustained and multi-vector campaign of legal, executive, and legislative pressure since at least September 2023. The chronology documented here draws entirely on the Tracker: Attempts to Neutralize the OSP, a primary source document recording 13 distinct actions.

The actions range from constitutional challenges filed at the Supreme Court, to petitions for the removal of the Special Prosecutor filed with both President Akufo-Addo and President Mahama, to a Parliamentary bill introduced in December 2025 to repeal Act 959 outright. A High Court ruling in April 2026 further complicated the OSP's operational independence by purporting to strip the OSP of its prosecutorial powers — a decision the OSP has indicated it will challenge.

Of the 13 recorded actions, 10 have been dismissed, withdrawn, or struck out. Two actions — the Supreme Court case (Adamtey v. AG) and one removal petition (Case 9) — remain unresolved. The April 2026 High Court ruling (Case 12) is subject to challenge.

Where relevant, the source document notes the cases under active OSP prosecution at the time of each challenge, suggesting a temporal correlation between high-profile investigations and the filing of neutralisation attempts.

Judicial / Constitutional
Multiple Supreme Court applications challenging the constitutional basis for OSP's independent prosecutorial powers under Act 959. One High Court ruling in April 2026 purported to nullify OSP's prosecutorial mandate, reversing prior convictions and ordering cases transferred to the AG.
Executive / Removal Petitions
Six formal petitions to remove the Special Prosecutor were received by the Presidency — two during Akufo-Addo's tenure and four under President Mahama. All resolved petitions were dismissed by the Chief Justice for lack of prima facie evidence.
Parliamentary / Legislative
In December 2025, the Majority Leader and Majority Chief Whip introduced a bill to repeal Act 959 and dissolve the OSP. Parliament's Speaker and a super-majority endorsed abolition. President Mahama subsequently requested withdrawal, describing the move as premature.
Civil Society Response
In April–May 2026, a coalition of 14 civil society organisations — including CDD-Ghana, Transparency International Ghana, and IMANI — filed to join the Adamtey v. AG Supreme Court case as Amici Curiae, supporting the constitutionality of the OSP's independent powers.

Supreme Court · Judicial
Kuranchie v. Attorney-General & OSP (1st Filing)
Petitioner: Kenneth Kwabena Agyei Kuranchie
Withdrawn

Nature of Action

Constitutional challenge filed at the Supreme Court invoking its exclusive original jurisdiction. The petitioner challenged the constitutionality of Act 959 and the OSP's creation and independence, arguing it violated various articles of the 1992 Constitution by granting independent prosecutorial powers and police-like authority outside direct Attorney-General control.

Relief Sought

The petitioner sought to place the OSP under the direct control of the Attorney-General, effectively dismantling its structural independence.

Chronology of Proceedings

Key Dates
September 2023: Suit filed at the Supreme Court.
June 2024: Plaintiff filed notice of discontinuance after failing to file statement of case for approximately 9 months.

Outcome

Withdrawn. The plaintiff discontinued the action voluntarily, without the merits being determined.

Cases Under Active Investigation at the Time

Cecilia Abena Dapaah (former Minister).

Constitutional Relevance

This case raised foundational questions about whether the OSP's institutional design — its independence from the Attorney-General — is consistent with the 1992 Constitution's framework for prosecutorial authority. The questions were not resolved on the merits at this stage.

5 Jul 2024 #03
Supreme Court · Judicial
Kuranchie v. Attorney-General & OSP (2nd Filing)
Petitioner: Kenneth Kwabena Agyei Kuranchie
Struck Out

Nature of Action

A refiled constitutional challenge at the Supreme Court, seeking identical relief to the discontinued first action (Case 1). Filed immediately following the notice of discontinuance of the prior suit.

Outcome

A seven-member panel of the Supreme Court, presided over by Chief Justice G. S. Torkornoo, struck out the writ without liberty to reapply, rendering the refiled case moot. The Court reviewed the notice of discontinuance and found the conduct abusive of court process.

"The OSP should not be encumbered by such lawsuits and must be allowed to fulfil its mandate." — Supreme Court of Ghana (7-member panel), 2024

Constitutional Relevance

The Supreme Court's ruling, with its explicit directive that the OSP not be encumbered by such suits, constitutes a significant institutional statement in favour of the OSP's operational independence. However, the merits of the constitutional challenge remained undecided.

Kuranchie Review Application
Petitioner: Kenneth Kwabena Agyei Kuranchie
Dismissed

Nature of Action

An application to review the Supreme Court's earlier decision that struck out the 2nd Filing without liberty to reapply (Case 3). The applicant sought to overturn the strike-out order and restore his ability to challenge the OSP's constitutionality.

Outcome

The full bench of the Supreme Court unanimously dismissed the review application on 11 December 2024 (source document lists this under the 2025 section; date is stated as 11 December 2024 in the text).

The Court ruled that the applicant's conduct — failing to file, then discontinuing — constituted a "blatant abuse" of court process. The applicant was barred from taking any further action on the matter. — Supreme Court of Ghana (Full Bench), 11 December 2024

Significance

This ruling represents the Supreme Court's most definitive statement against the series of litigation efforts by this particular actor. The characterisation of the conduct as a "blatant abuse" of court process, and the complete bar on further applications, signals judicial impatience with instrumentalised constitutional challenges against the OSP.

8 Dec 2025 #11
Parliament · Repeal Bill
Private Member's Bill to Repeal the OSP Act (Act 959)
Introduced by: Mahama Ayariga (Majority Leader) & Rockson Dafeamekpor (Majority Chief Whip)
Withdrawn

Nature of Action

A Private Member's Bill introduced in Parliament by the Majority Leader, Mahama Ayariga, and the Majority Chief Whip, Rockson Dafeamekpor, seeking to repeal the Office of the Special Prosecutor Act, 2017 (Act 959) and dissolve the Office.

Stated Justifications

The legislators cited structural weakness in the OSP's design, constitutional overlaps, duplicated functions vis-à-vis other anti-corruption agencies, and high operational costs.

Chronology

Key Dates
6 December 2025: Speaker of Parliament Alban Bagbin, together with Members of Parliament (described in the source as a "super majority"), unanimously agreed to recommend the abolition of the OSP.
8 December 2025: The Majority Leader and Majority Chief Whip introduced the repeal bill in Parliament.
11 December 2025: President John Mahama requested the withdrawal of the bill, describing it as premature.

Outcome

Withdrawn on the instructions of President Mahama.

Significance

This is the only recorded instance of a legislative attempt to repeal the OSP's founding statute. The rapidity of the sequence — parliamentary endorsement of abolition, introduction of the bill, and presidential withdrawal — within five days is notable. The source document associates this event with the arrest of Martin Kpebu.

High Court · Constitutional
Peter Archibald Hyde v. OSP (Rice Scandal Defendant)
Petitioner: Peter Archibald Hyde (defendant in Republic v. Issah Seidu & 3 Ors)
Ruling vs. OSP

Nature of Action

A constitutional challenge filed at the Accra High Court by a defendant in an active OSP prosecution — the "Rice Scandal" case (The Republic v. Issah Seidu & 3 Ors). The challenge was to the constitutional basis for the OSP's prosecutorial powers under Article 88 of the 1992 Constitution.

Detailed Chronology

Key Dates
30 January 2026: Application filed at the Accra High Court.
Feb–Mar 2026: Parties file statements of case. OSP raises a preliminary objection — lack of jurisdiction and pendency of Supreme Court case (Adamtey v. AG, J1/3/2026).
9 March 2026: Judge stays proceedings pending the Supreme Court's determination in the Adamtey case.
8 April 2026: Applicant prepares and files his own hearing notice.
9 April 2026: Registrar issues the applicant-prepared hearing notice (unchanged).
14 April 2026: Hearing notice served on OSP for 8:00 a.m.
15 April 2026: Judge delivers ruling, effectively reversing the earlier stay. Judge pronounces that the OSP lacks prosecutorial powers and has not been properly mandated to prosecute; reverses all previous convictions obtained by the OSP; orders all OSP-initiated cases transferred to the AG's department.
15 April 2026: OSP announces it is taking steps to overturn the decision, noting the High Court does not have jurisdiction to strike down parts of an Act of Parliament as unconstitutional.
16 April 2026: Deputy AG Justice Srem-Sai announces readiness of the AG to take over OSP cases from the following day.

Outcome

High Court ruling against the OSP, subject to challenge. The OSP stated it would seek to overturn the ruling. The decision has significant immediate operational consequences given the AG's announced readiness to assume OSP cases.

"The OSP stated that it is taking steps to quickly overturn the decision of the General Jurisdiction Court since the High Court does not have jurisdiction to, in effect, strike down parts of an Act of Parliament as unconstitutional." — OSP Statement, 15 April 2026

Constitutional Relevance

This ruling, if it stands, would have the most immediate practical effect on OSP operations of any action in this chronology. However, the OSP's position — that the High Court lacked jurisdiction to perform what is effectively a constitutional review — is a substantial legal argument. The pending Adamtey v. AG case at the Supreme Court raises the same core issues.
